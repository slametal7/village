Brookfield Village, Oakland, California

Overview and Location

Brookfield Village is a residential neighborhood located in East Oakland, California, approximately 5 miles southeast of downtown Oakland and adjacent to Oakland International Airport. The neighborhood is bounded roughly by Hegenberger Road to the north, Doolittle Drive to the south, 82nd Avenue to the west, and 106th Avenue to the east. The main thoroughfares are 98th Avenue and Edes Avenue, which serve as the commercial and social hubs of the community.

This working-class neighborhood features primarily single-family homes built in the 1940s and 1950s, with tree-lined streets and a suburban feel uncommon in much of urban Oakland. The area covers approximately 0.7 square miles and sits at an elevation of 30 feet above sea level, with relatively flat terrain typical of Oakland's eastern neighborhoods.

Historical Development

Brookfield Village was developed during World War II (1941-1945) in response to the massive influx of workers needed for the Bay Area's booming war industries. The neighborhood was constructed on land that had previously been zoned for industrial uses but was repurposed for urgent housing needs during the wartime housing shortage.

The development was spearheaded by Albert Bernhardt and the Stoneon Brothers, who envisioned creating a planned community that would offer quality housing for wartime workers and their families. The project comprised over 1,200 homes built in the then-popular "tract house" style, making it one of the first large-scale suburban-style developments in Oakland.

Key historical aspects of Brookfield Village's development include:

  • Architectural Style: Primarily modest 2-3 bedroom bungalows with post-war utilitarian design
  • Street Layout: Innovative winding, contoured streets designed to break from the grid pattern common in older Oakland neighborhoods
  • Landscaping: Extensive planting of shade trees that have matured into the neighborhood's distinctive canopy
  • Marketing: Advertised as "a model village on the Pacific Coast" emphasizing modern amenities and community planning

Notable Residents

Brookfield Village's most famous former resident is Damian Lillard, the NBA superstar who grew up in the neighborhood before becoming:

  • 6-time NBA All-Star
  • NBA Rookie of the Year (2013)
  • Olympic gold medalist (2021)
  • Milwaukee Bucks point guard (as of 2023)

Lillard has frequently mentioned Brookfield Village in interviews, crediting his childhood experiences there with shaping his work ethic and competitive spirit. Other notable figures associated with the neighborhood include:

  • M.C. Hammer - The famous rapper and entertainer spent part of his childhood in Brookfield Village
  • Gary Payton II - NBA player and son of Hall of Famer Gary Payton attended schools in the area
  • Various local musicians - Several Bay Area hip-hop artists have roots in Brookfield Village

Community Features and Amenities

Brookfield Village offers residents several important community resources:

Parks and Recreation

  • Brookfield Park: The neighborhood's central green space featuring playgrounds, basketball courts, and picnic areas
  • 98th Avenue Community Garden: A thriving urban garden project
  • Walking Trails: Several maintained paths along neighborhood streets

Commercial Areas

The main commercial corridor along 98th Avenue includes:

  • Local grocery stores and markets
  • Family-owned restaurants featuring diverse cuisines
  • Small retail shops and service businesses
  • Community health clinic

Transportation

Brookfield Village benefits from excellent transportation access:

  • Public Transit: Served by AC Transit bus lines 40 and 98
  • Freeway Access: Close to I-880 (Nimitz Freeway)
  • Airport Proximity: Just 1.5 miles from Oakland International Airport
  • Bike Lanes: Designated bike routes on several streets

Architecture and Housing

The neighborhood's architectural character reflects its World War II-era origins:

  • Housing Types: Primarily single-story bungalows and duplexes
  • Lot Sizes: Typically 5,000-6,000 square feet
  • Architectural Features: Modest front porches, detached garages, and small front yards
  • Construction: Primarily wood-frame construction with stucco or wood siding

In recent years, some original homes have been expanded or remodeled, while others remain largely unchanged from their original construction. The neighborhood has seen gradual gentrification pressures common throughout the Bay Area, though less intense than in areas closer to downtown Oakland.

Community Organizations

Several active organizations serve Brookfield Village residents:

  • Brookfield Village Neighborhood Association: Addresses quality-of-life issues and organizes events
  • East Oakland Youth Development Center: Provides after-school and summer programs
  • 98th Avenue Merchants Association: Supports local businesses
  • Brookfield Village Community Church: Hosts food drives and community meals

Challenges and Opportunities

Like many East Oakland neighborhoods, Brookfield Village faces certain challenges:

  • Economic Disparities: Some residents struggle with rising costs of living
  • Public Safety: Occasional property crimes typical of urban areas
  • Infrastructure: Aging streets and utilities needing maintenance

However, the neighborhood also has significant strengths:

  • Strong Community Bonds: Multi-generational families and long-term residents
  • Strategic Location: Proximity to employment centers and transportation
  • Affordable Housing: Relative to other Bay Area communities
  • Cultural Diversity: Rich mix of African American, Latino, and Asian influences

Future Development

Several planned developments may impact Brookfield Village in coming years:

  • Oakland Airport expansion projects
  • Planned improvements to Hegenberger Road corridor
  • Potential new housing developments on vacant parcels
  • Proposed community center renovations

The neighborhood's community organizations are actively engaged in planning processes to ensure future development benefits existing residents while maintaining the area's unique character.
